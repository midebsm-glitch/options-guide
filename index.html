Overview

An option is a contract — not a stock

When you buy a stock, you own a piece of a company. When you buy an option, you own a contract that gives you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell that stock at a fixed price before a deadline.

Real-world analogy: You find a house for $300,000. You pay the seller $5,000 to "reserve" it at that price for 90 days. If prices rise to $350,000, you lock in the lower price. If they drop, you walk away — losing only your $5,000. That reservation fee is your option premium.
Buyer

Option buyer

Pays a premium upfront. Gets the right to buy or sell shares. Max loss = premium paid. Potential gains can be large.

Seller

Option seller

Collects the premium upfront. Obligated to fulfill if the buyer exercises. Max gain = premium. Risk can be unlimited.

Why trade options?
🛡

Hedging — protect shares you already own from a market downturn without selling them.

⚡

Leverage — control 100 shares for a fraction of the cost. A $500 option can move like $15,000 in stock.

💰

Income — sell options repeatedly to collect premium as a recurring income stream.

🎯

Speculation — bet on direction, volatility, or time with a clearly defined max loss.

Call option

The right to BUY shares

You buy a call when you believe a stock will go up. It grants the right to purchase 100 shares at the strike price — even if the market price is higher.

Example: Apple trades at $180. You buy a call with a $185 strike for $4/share ($400 total). If Apple rises to $200, you buy at $185 — profit is $200 − $185 − $4 = $11/share ($1,100). If Apple stays below $185, you lose only your $400 premium.
📅

Today: Pay $400 premium. You own the right to buy 100 Apple shares at $185.

✅

Stock rises above $185: Option is "in the money." Sell the contract for a profit or exercise it.

✗

Stock stays below $185: Option expires worthless. You lose the $400. Nothing more.

Put option

The right to SELL shares

You buy a put when you believe a stock will go down. It grants the right to sell 100 shares at the strike price — even if the market is lower.

Example: Tesla is at $250. You buy a put with a $240 strike for $5/share ($500 total). If Tesla falls to $200, you sell at $240 — profit is $240 − $200 − $5 = $35/share ($3,500). If Tesla stays above $240, you lose your $500.

Glossary

10 terms every options trader must know

Strike price
The locked-in price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put) the stock, no matter what the market is doing.
Premium
What you pay for the contract. One contract = 100 shares, so a $5 premium costs $500 total.
Expiration date
The deadline. After this, the option is either exercised, sold, or expires worthless. No extensions.
In the money (ITM)
A call is ITM when stock > strike. A put is ITM when stock < strike. Has real intrinsic value right now.
Out of the money (OTM)
The option has no intrinsic value yet. Cheaper to buy, but may expire worthless if the stock doesn't move enough.
At the money (ATM)
Strike price equals (or is very close to) the current stock price. Highest sensitivity to moves.
Delta (Δ)
How much the option moves per $1 change in stock. Delta 0.5 = the option gains $0.50 for every $1 the stock gains.
Theta (Θ)
Time decay — the daily cost of holding an option. Options lose value every single day as expiration approaches.
Implied volatility
The market's estimate of future price swings. High IV = expensive options. Spikes before earnings announcements.
Exercise
Actually using the contract to buy/sell shares at the strike. Most traders just sell the option contract instead.

Risk management

5 risks every beginner must understand

RiskWhat it meansHow to manage it
⏳ Time decay Options lose value every day. A flat stock still costs you money. Buy options with 30–60+ days to expiry. Avoid 0–7 day contracts as a beginner.
💀 100% loss An option can expire completely worthless. Every dollar of premium is gone. Never risk money you can't afford to lose entirely. Start with small positions.
♾️ Naked selling Selling uncovered calls = unlimited potential loss if the stock rockets. Beginners: only sell covered calls (you must own the underlying shares first).
💥 Volatility crush IV collapses after earnings even if you're right on direction — option loses value. Understand implied volatility before trading around events like earnings reports.
⚡ Leverage cuts both ways A 5% stock move can mean a 50%+ option move — in either direction. Size each trade to risk ≤ 1–2% of your total account. Discipline above all.
Golden rules

6 rules for beginner options traders

  • 01
    Paper trade first — use a simulator with fake money until you're consistently profitable before risking real capital.
  • 02
    Only trade options on stocks you already follow and understand. Don't trade what you don't know.
  • 03
    Start by buying calls and puts only. Avoid selling options until you have real experience.
  • 04
    Set a max loss before entering every trade. Exit automatically when you hit it. No exceptions.
  • 05
    Avoid earnings plays until you understand implied volatility and volatility crush cold.
  • 06
    Never risk more than 2–5% of your total account on a single options trade. Protect your capital first.
